PART I — SUBMISSIONS ON BEHALF OF THE PETITIONER

A. Preliminary Statutory Framework: The Architecture of Section 79

The Petitioner respectfully submits that the starting point for this Court's analysis must be a careful and purposive reading of Section 79 of the IT Act in its entirety. Section 79(1) provides the general rule of exemption: an intermediary shall not be liable for any third party information, data, or communication link made available or hosted by it. However, this grant of immunity is expressly conditional upon compliance with the requirements in sub-section (2), and is expressly withdrawn by the conditions in sub-section (3).
Section 79(2) conditions safe harbour on three cumulative requirements. First, the intermediary's function must be limited to providing access to a communication system over which information made available by third parties is transmitted or temporarily stored. Second, the intermediary must not have initiated the transmission. Third, the intermediary must not have selected the receiver of the transmission, and must not have selected or modified the information contained in the transmission. These three requirements are conjunctive, not alternative. Failure to satisfy any one of them extinguishes the immunity.
Section 79(3) further provides that the exemption shall not apply where the intermediary has conspired with or abetted or aided or induced, whether by threats or promise or otherwise in the commission of the unlawful act, or upon receiving actual knowledge, or on being notified by the appropriate Government or its agency that any information, data or communication link residing in or connected to a computer resource controlled by the intermediary is being used to commit the unlawful act, the intermediary fails to expeditiously remove or disable access to that material on that resource without vitiating the evidence in any manner.
It is the Petitioner's submission that DeepSearch Inc. satisfies none of the conditions in Section 79(2) and has squarely triggered the exceptions in Section 79(3). The IT (Intermediary Guidelines and Digital Media Ethics Code) Rules, 2021 ("Intermediary Rules 2021") separately impose a mandatory due diligence framework, the violation of which independently extinguishes safe harbour protection, regardless of the Section 79(2) analysis.

B. The Personality Model Is an Active Content Creation Engine, Not a Passive Conduit

i. The Fundamental Distinction Between Hosting and Generating

The entire edifice of safe harbour protection rests upon the concept of the intermediary as a passive technical conduit. This principle has been recognised and refined across both Indian and international jurisprudence. The Hon'ble Delhi High Court in Christian Louboutin SAS v. Nakul Bajaj & Ors., 2018 SCC OnLine Del 12220, drew the foundational distinction with clarity, holding that Section 79 protects only those entities whose role is "technical, automatic and passive" and who act "without knowledge or control" over the content they carry. The Court was unambiguous that the safe harbour "cannot be abused by extending such protection to those persons who were not intermediaries and were active participants in unlawful acts."
The Personality Model of DeepSearch fails this test at the most fundamental level. Unlike a file-hosting service, a search engine, or a social media platform — all of which receive and store content created by human users — the Personality Model synthesises content that would otherwise not exist. When a user invokes the Rachel Personality Model, no pre-existing video of Rachel performing a sexual act is retrieved or hosted. Instead, DeepSearch's AI architecture processes biometric parameters encoded in the model — Rachel's facial geometry, vocal characteristics, movement patterns, and behavioural cues — and generates an entirely new audio-visual product that appears to show Rachel engaging in the requested act. The creative and technical substance of the harmful output is contributed not by the user but by the model itself. The user provides only a prompt; DeepSearch provides the simulated person.
This Court should note with precision what the Personality Model is: it is not a tool that edits a user's own footage. It is a trained neural architecture that encodes the biometric identity of a specific named individual and can produce unlimited novel depictions of that individual in any scenario a user specifies. It is, in functional legal terms, a synthetic identity engine. The distinction between hosting user content and generating identity-based synthetic outputs is not merely technical — it is the very line that Section 79 draws between immunity and liability.

ii. The Active Participant Doctrine — Indian Jurisprudence

The principle that active participation in the generation or facilitation of unlawful content forfeits safe harbour protection is well-established in Indian law. In Christian Louboutin (supra), the Delhi High Court held that a platform that curates, certifies, promotes, and structures commercial content — rather than passively hosting seller listings — becomes an "active participant" and loses Section 79 protection. The ratio of this judgment is that where a platform exercises control over the character of content, rather than merely providing technical infrastructure for its transmission, it cannot claim the immunity reserved for neutral conduits.
In the present case, DeepSearch exercises far greater control than the e-commerce platform in Christian Louboutin. The platform did not merely host listings that happened to involve a luxury brand; it structured the commercial relationship. DeepSearch does not merely host content that happens to use Rachel's likeness; it builds and maintains the Rachel Personality Model in its global library, ensuring its technical availability for any user globally to generate content using her synthetic identity. The act of constructing, training, and persistently maintaining a model indexed to a specific individual's biometric identity is, on any reasonable reading, an act of active participation in the content that model enables.
This reasoning is reinforced by the decision in MySpace Inc. v. Super Cassettes Industries Ltd., 2016 SCC OnLine Del 6382, where the Delhi High Court, while reaffirming the general principle of intermediary protection, was careful to limit that protection to platforms that act upon specific notice and that do not independently participate in the creation or curation of infringing content. The Court's observation that "actual knowledge and not mere suspicion is essential to fasten liability" is significant not because it insulates DeepSearch — for reasons addressed below, actual knowledge was established here — but because its contra-factual implication is that where a platform does more than passively host, and where knowledge is demonstrated, the full weight of liability follows.
iii. The European Standard — Active Role and Economic Integration

The Court of Justice of the European Union in L'Oréal SA v. eBay International AG, [2011] ECJ C-324/09, a judgment of persuasive authority in Indean courts interpreting analogous safe harbour provisions, articulated the principle that a platform loses immunity where it plays an "active role" in promoting, optimising, or facilitating access to infringing content. The CJEU held that eBay's use of keyword advertising, its algorithmic structuring of listings, and its commercial integration with the presentation of infringing goods demonstrated an active commercial engagement that displaced passive conduit status. The Court observed that eBay's operations went "beyond mere neutrality" because it structured and promoted the listings rather than simply hosting them.
Similarly, the European Court of Human Rights in Delfi AS v. Estonia, App. No. 64569/09 [2015] ECtHR, upheld the imposition of publisher liability on a news portal for user comments, holding that the platform had "integrated the comments section into its business model" and had "determined which of the comments would be published and which would not." The ECHR found that the platform's economic dependence on engagement, its structural control over the comment environment, and its failure to proactively prevent manifestly unlawful content displaced its claim to passive intermediary status. The Court's observation that "the number of visits and revenue depends on the number of comments" directly parallels DeepSearch's commercial model: the Personality Model is a premium feature that generates engagement and revenue, and the Rachel Personality Model exists in DeepSearch's library as a product offering, not a passive occurrence.
Applying Delfi to the present facts, it is submitted that DeepSearch's global library of Personality Models — including the Rachel Personality Model — represents precisely the kind of commercial integration of harmful content into a revenue-generating business model that forfeits passive intermediary status. The platform does not incidentally host user-generated depictions of Rachel; it purposefully creates, maintains, and commercially offers a synthetic Rachel for user deployment.

C. Section 79(2) Is Violated on All Three Counts

Even if this Court were to treat DeepSearch as an intermediary in the general sense, safe harbour under Section 79(1) cannot be claimed because each of the three conditions in Section 79(2) is independently violated.

i. Initiation of Transmission

Section 79(2)(b)(i) requires that the intermediary must not have initiated the transmission. Initiation must be understood purposively, consistent with the principle articulated in Spentex Industries Ltd. v. Commissioner of Central Excise, (2015) 15 SCC 291, where this Hon'ble Court held that statutory provisions must be interpreted in alignment with the "overall scheme and purpose of the law" and that a "literal interpretation that leads to absurdity" must be avoided. Interpreting "initiation" to encompass only the moment a user clicks "generate" — while ignoring the prior acts of model construction, biometric data encoding, and library listing — would lead to precisely the kind of purposive absurdity that Spentex cautions against.
The substantive initiation in this case is DeepSearch's act of building the Rachel Personality Model, encoding her biometric identity within it, listing it in a globally accessible library, and thereby offering to the world the capability to generate synthetic Rachel-attributed content. That act — not the user's subsequent prompt — is the point of initiation, for without it, no synthetic content targeting Rachel could be generated at all. DeepSearch did not wait for user demand to build the Rachel Personality Model; it constructed and deployed it in anticipation of, and to fulfil, demand. That is initiation in the clearest sense.
ii. Selection of Content and Receiver

Section 79(2)(b)(ii) requires that the intermediary must not have selected either the receiver of the transmission or the content. The Personality Model architecture constitutes a built-in selection mechanism. The selection of Rachel Sharma as the synthetic subject of the model was made by DeepSearch when it constructed, trained, and listed the Rachel Personality Model. This is not a neutral technical act; it is a deliberate commercial decision to make Rachel's biometric identity available as a synthesis target for any user globally.
The argument that this is merely "responding to user demand" cannot survive scrutiny. A platform that proactively builds and maintains a library indexed to specific named individuals has made the fundamental selection — the selection of identity targets — that distinguishes it from a neutral tool. A general video-generation AI that does not maintain a library of named individuals' biometric models is neutral; DeepSearch's Personality Model library, which specifically indexes real, identified people, is by design a selective system, and that selectivity is DeepSearch's own, not the user's.
iii. Modification of Information

Section 79(2)(b)(iii) requires that the intermediary must not have modified the information. The Petitioner submits that this condition, read purposively, is most dramatically violated by the Personality Model. Traditional "modification" — such as a platform's compression or format-conversion of uploaded files — is minimal and technical. The Personality Model performs wholesale synthetic fabrication: it does not take Rachel's real footage and alter it; it takes biometric parameters derived from her footage and generates entirely new audio-visual content — content that was never created, never existed, and never occurred — in which she appears to do things she has never done.
This is not modification of existing information. It is the creation of false information using a real person's identity as raw material. Purposively construed, this represents the most extreme form of information modification contemplated by Section 79(2)(b)(iii), and no reading of that provision consistent with its protective purpose can accommodate it within the safe harbour.

D. Section 79(3): Actual Knowledge and Failure to Act

Independent of the Section 79(2) analysis, safe harbour is extinguished under Section 79(3)(b) by reason of DeepSearch's failure to act upon receiving actual and specific knowledge that the Rachel Personality Model was being used to commit unlawful acts against an Indean citizen.
The facts are unambiguous. Following the filing of the cyber complaint at Mohini Police Station, Indean authorities formally notified DeepSearch of: (i) the existence and specific identity of the Rachel Personality Model; (ii) its confirmed use to generate non-consensual sexual deepfake content targeting a named Indean citizen; and (iii) the consequent criminal complaint and regulatory proceedings. This constitutes actual knowledge in the most direct and unambiguous sense — it is not constructive notice, not algorithmic flag detection, but direct governmental identification of a specific, named model causing specific, documented harm to a specific, identified individual.
The standard for actual knowledge applicable to this situation was addressed by the Delhi High Court in Google LLC v. Visaka Industries, where the Court held that "actual knowledge must be through a court order or Government agency." That standard is met here precisely: it was Government authorities, not a private complaint alone, who notified DeepSearch. This is not a case of generalized suspicion or algorithmic inference; it is formal governmental notification of a specific unlawful model.
DeepSearch's response to this notification was to refuse removal, invoking its "terms of use" policy of "uninterrupted services" and claiming that removing the Rachel Personality Model would undermine its "customer credibility." This refusal is a textbook violation of Section 79(3)(b). The provision requires expeditious removal upon receiving actual knowledge. DeepSearch did not merely delay; it categorically refused. No contractual terms of use can override an explicit statutory obligation; to hold otherwise would allow private contractual arrangements to nullify parliamentary enactments, a proposition that finds no support in law.
Furthermore, the Intermediary Rules 2021 impose a separate and independent layer of compliance obligations. Rule 3(1)(b)(i) requires intermediaries to ensure their platform is not used to publish or share content that is "obscene" within the meaning of Section 67 of the IT Act. Rule 3(1)(b)(iv) prohibits content that is "harmful to minors" or that violates the dignity of any person. Rule 3(1)(b)(vii) prohibits content that threatens the unity, integrity, defence or security of the country, or that infringes any patent, trademark, copyright or other proprietary rights. The Rachel Personality Model, by enabling the generation of non-consensual sexual content using a real person's likeness, directly violates Rules 3(1)(b)(i) and 3(1)(b)(iv). DeepSearch's failure to comply with its due diligence obligations under the Intermediary Rules 2021 independently strips any residual safe harbour it might otherwise claim.

E. The Architecture of DeepSearch Inherently Enables Unlawful Outputs, Attracting Independent Design Liability

It is submitted that the liability of DeepSearch arises not merely from user misuse but from the very architecture of its system. The Personality Model feature is specifically designed to replicate real individuals with minimal data input — requiring only 30 seconds of existing footage to construct a complete synthetic identity model. This design choice makes the creation of non-consensual deepfakes both foreseeable and, in practical terms, inevitable. Where a system is architected in a manner that predictably produces unlawful outputs, liability must attach at the design stage itself. This Hon'ble Court ought to recognise that the illegality here is embedded not only in the final output but in the technological framework that enables it.
The principle that liability may arise from systemic design choices — not merely from specific outputs — was given authoritative support in the United States District Court's analysis in Doe v. GitHub Inc., N.D. Cal. 2023. In that case, the Court recognised that AI models trained on copyrighted data "without programming to treat attribution, copyright notices, and license terms as legally essential" demonstrate systemic design liability; it accepted that where an AI system is "intentionally designed" to suppress legally significant protections, and where there is a "substantial risk" that the system will produce violative outputs at scale, liability arises from the design itself, not merely from individual instances of harm. The Court's reasoning — that the training and deployment architecture can ground independent liability — applies with full force to the present case.
DeepSearch's Personality Model was designed with each of the following architecture choices that individually, and cumulatively, demonstrate design defect: (i) a 30-second footage threshold for identity cloning, deliberately minimising the barrier to replication; (ii) persistence in a globally accessible library without access controls or consent verification; (iii) no mechanism requiring the biometric subject's consent before a Personality Model is constructed and listed; (iv) no takedown mechanism allowing the biometric subject to remove their own model; (v) no automated content screening for sexual or degrading output categories. Each of these design choices made non-consensual identity exploitation more, not less, likely. Reasonable alternative designs exist at minimal technical cost. The absence of even elementary consent verification reveals that DeepSearch's commercial incentives were prioritised over the fundamental rights and dignity of the individuals whose biometric identities were being encoded and commercialised.
This argument draws support from the scholarship of Richards and Hartzog, who in their foundational work A Duty of Loyalty for Privacy Law, 99 Washington University Law Review 961 (2021), argue compellingly that data-collecting entities owe a duty of loyalty to those whose personal information they handle, and that "loyalty would manifest itself primarily as a prohibition on designing digital tools and processing data in a way that conflicts with a trusting party's best interests." They observe that "data collectors bound by such a duty of loyalty would be obligated to act in the best interests of the people exposing their data." DeepSearch's Personality Model architecture is the paradigm case of the kind of conflicted, self-serving design the authors identify: it harvests Rachel's biometric identity for commercial gain while being architecturally indifferent to the catastrophic harm its misuse predictably causes. This Court is invited to recognise that such deliberate design choices ground independent liability.

F. AI Co-Authorship — DeepSearch Is a Functional Co-Creator of the Harmful Content

The Petitioner invites this Court to adopt the principle of functional co-authorship as the appropriate doctrinal framework for assessing AI-generated harm. This principle holds that liability under Section 79 must be assessed by reference to who functionally created the harmful content — not merely who formally uploaded or triggered it.
In the present case, the authorship of the deepfake is not Mike Rathore's alone. Rathore provided a prompt. DeepSearch's Personality Model provided the entirety of the synthetic representation: Rachel's face, voice, facial geometry, mannerisms, audio characteristics, and behavioural patterns — all derived from the model's biometric encoding. These elements are not user-generated; they are platform-generated. The dominant creative and technical contribution to the harmful output was DeepSearch's, not the user's. Under the functional co-authorship principle, where the platform contributes the dominant elements of the content's harmful character — the synthetic identity of the victim, the believable fabrication of her appearance and voice — the platform cannot claim the passive immunity of a conduit.
Professor Danielle Keats Citron, in her landmark article "Sexual Privacy", 128 Yale Law Journal 1870 (2019), articulates the particular harm inflicted by deepfake sexual content and the inadequacy of existing legal frameworks in addressing it. She observes that non-consensual deepfake pornography "undermines individuals' ability to control their own sexual narratives" and causes "grave harms to people's intimate lives, careers, and mental health." Citron and Franks, in "Criminalizing Revenge Porn", 49 Wake Forest Law Review 345 (2014), further demonstrate that the harm of such content is inseparable from the technological capacity that enables its creation. Applied here: the harm to Rachel is inseparable from DeepSearch's Personality Model. Without the model, there is no plausible synthetic Rachel. The model is not incidental to the harm; the model is the harm's infrastructure.
The European Union's regulatory response confirms this analysis. The EU AI Act, 2024 (Articles 5 and 6, read with Annex III) classifies biometric identification and manipulation systems that can generate synthetic representations of identifiable individuals as "high-risk AI systems." Such systems are subject to mandatory conformity assessments, transparency obligations, and human oversight requirements. DeepSearch's Personality Model falls squarely within this high-risk classification. While the EU AI Act operates as a regulatory instrument rather than a liability statute, its classification demonstrates the global consensus that biometric identity manipulation systems cannot be treated as neutral tools, and that their developers bear heightened responsibilities for the foreseeable harms they enable.

G. Failure of the Non-Infringing Use Test and the Neutrality Requirement

Even on the most generous reading of the safe harbour framework — one that adopted the substantial non-infringing use standard articulated in Sony Corp. of America v. Universal City Studios Inc., 464 U.S. 417 (1984), as the benchmark for technology neutrality — DeepSearch's Personality Model cannot satisfy this test with respect to the specific feature at issue.
The Sony Court held that technology is protected where it is "capable of substantial non-infringing uses." This test requires both design neutrality and use neutrality. The Personality Model fails both. On design: unlike a general generative AI tool that creates original synthetic characters, the Personality Model is specifically engineered to produce synthetic representations of identified real individuals from minimal biometric input. The very architecture — clone-from-30-seconds, persistent library indexed to named persons — is identity-targeted, not neutral. On use: when the question is posed directly — what are the substantial non-infringing uses of a tool that clones a specific private individual's identity from 30 seconds of footage and makes that clone available globally — the answer is self-evidently inadequate to satisfy the Sony standard. Unlike the VCR in Sony (which had demonstrably extensive and commercially significant legitimate uses), the Personality Model's commercial appeal is structurally built on the appropriation of real individuals' identities, which is the very source of potential harm.
Rónán Kennedy, in +-"Artificial Intelligence and Intermediary Liability", Computer Law & Security Review (2023), identifies precisely this limitation, observing that the passive conduit analogy breaks down entirely when applied to AI systems that do not merely carry or host content but synthesise it using encoded representations of real persons. Marietje Schaake et al., in "Regulating AI: The Human Rights Dimension", Oxford Internet Institute (2021), similarly argue that AI systems generating synthetic identity content occupy a fundamentally different legal category from neutral hosting services and require human rights-calibrated liability frameworks.

H. Training and Deployment Violate the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 and Emerging Copyright Norms

It is submitted that the training and deployment of the Personality Model independently violate emerging principles governing generative AI, data protection, and copyright that are recognised both under Indean law and as persuasive comparative authority.
The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 ("DPDPA") — the Indean analog of comprehensive data protection legislation — establishes a consent-based framework for the processing of personal data. Rachel's biometric data, including her facial geometry, vocal characteristics, and movement patterns, constitutes sensitive personal data. The construction of the Rachel Personality Model required the processing of this data without Rachel's knowledge, consent, or any lawful basis under the DPDPA. DeepSearch did not obtain Rachel's consent before training its model on her footage; it did not provide her with a notice of processing; it did not establish a legitimate purpose for retaining a persistent biometric clone of her identity in a globally accessible commercial library. Each of these failures constitutes an independent violation of the DPDPA's consent framework.
The training of generative AI systems on personal data and likeness without consent raises serious legal concerns, as recognised in the Working Paper on Generative AI and Copyright issued by the Ministry of Commerce and Industry of Indea. The use of publicly available data for training purposes does not automatically negate the requirement of consent, particularly where such data pertains to identifiable individuals. Rachel's social media presence — the footage from which her Personality Model was constructed — was shared in the context of her professional persona as a content creator. That contextual sharing did not constitute consent to the biometric encoding of her identity into a synthetic replication engine. The replication of personality and likeness through AI-generated outputs constitutes an infringement of her personality rights and potentially violates copyright norms governing text and data mining. The absence of a clear statutory framework does not absolve the platform of liability, especially where the resulting outputs cause demonstrable harm.
The reasoning in Doe v. GitHub Inc. (supra) is again directly applicable: the Court's recognition that training AI models "without programming to treat attribution, copyright notices, and license terms as legally essential" demonstrates systemic liability extends, by analogy, to DeepSearch's training of its Personality Model without programming consent verification, identity-subject rights, or takedown mechanisms for the biometric subject. The systemic omission of rights-protecting safeguards at the design and training stage grounds liability independently of any individual harmful output.
Under the EU Digital Services Act, 2022 (Articles 16, 26, and 28), very large online platforms and search engines are subject to enhanced obligations including risk assessment for systemic risks to fundamental rights, prohibition of dark patterns, and transparency in algorithmic systems. DeepSearch's global scale and the systemic risk its Personality Model poses to the dignity and privacy of the individuals whose biometric identities it encodes align precisely with the category of systemic risk contemplated by the DSA. While not directly applicable as statute, the DSA represents the emerging global regulatory standard for AI-generated harm and constitutes strong persuasive authority for this Court's purposive interpretation of Section 79.

I. Specific Offence Provisions of the IT Act Apply to DeepSearch's Conduct

It is submitted that independent of the Section 79 analysis, DeepSearch's conduct engages specific criminal provisions of the IT Act that do not admit of safe harbour. Section 66E of the IT Act prohibits the intentional capture, publication, or transmission of an image of a private area of any person without consent, in circumstances violating the privacy of that person. The Rachel Personality Model enabled the generation and dissemination of non-consensual sexual imagery of Rachel — a direct violation of Section 66E. Section 66C prohibits identity theft, and Section 66D prohibits cheating by personation using a computer resource. The Personality Model is architecturally designed to enable users to generate content "as Rachel" — to impersonate her through synthetic means — engaging both provisions.
Section 67 of the IT Act prohibits the publication or transmission of obscene material in electronic form. Section 67A prohibits publication of material containing sexually explicit acts. Section 67B addresses content depicting persons in obscene acts. The deepfake video generated using DeepSearch's Personality Model constitutes sexually explicit material published in electronic form and directly engages Sections 67, 67A, and 67B. DeepSearch's refusal to remove the Rachel Personality Model even after receiving governmental notice of its use to generate such content renders it a knowing facilitator of these offences. Safe harbour does not extend to facilitate the commission of specific criminal offences under the IT Act, as the plain reading of Section 79(3) and the scheme of the Act make clear.
The protective purpose of these provisions finds additional support in comparative law. California's AB 602 (2019) and AB 730 (2019) specifically created causes of action for the use of AI-generated deepfakes in non-consensual sexual content and political disinformation respectively. The UK Online Safety Act, 2023 — Section 66 on intimate image abuse and Clause 164 on deepfakes — similarly imposes specific liability for the production and distribution of non-consensual synthetic intimate imagery. These legislative developments reflect the emerging global consensus that generative AI tools used to create non-consensual sexual deepfakes cannot shelter behind general-purpose intermediary immunity frameworks.
