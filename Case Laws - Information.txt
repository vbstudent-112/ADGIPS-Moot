Indian Case Laws: Petitioner
1. Christian Louboutin v. Nakul Bajaj
Explanatory Paragraph
It is submitted that intermediary protection under Section 79 is unavailable where the platform plays an active role in facilitating or shaping unlawful content, thereby losing its status as a neutral conduit. In Christian Louboutin v. Nakul Bajaj, the Hon’ble Delhi High Court held that safe harbour cannot be extended to entities that are not merely passive intermediaries but are “active participants” in the underlying unlawful activity. The Court emphasized that intermediary status requires the platform to remain technical, automatic, and passive, without exercising control or influence over the content. This principle directly applies to the present case, where DeepSearch, through its generative architecture and Personality Model, does not merely host or transmit content but actively creates and structures synthetic outputs, including identity-based deepfakes. Such transformation of user input into new expressive content demonstrates a level of control and participation that is incompatible with the passive role required under Section 79. Therefore, DeepSearch cannot claim safe harbour and must be treated as a content creator/publisher, attracting full liability.
Key Extracts (Use in Memorial)
    • “Section 79… cannot be abused by extending such protection to those persons who were not intermediaries and were active participants in unlawful Act.” 
    • Intermediary must be “technical, automatic and passive… without knowledge or control.” 
    • Platforms that play an active role in facilitating transactions/content lose immunity 

International Case Laws: Petitioner
L'Oréal SA v. eBay
Explanatory Paragraph
It is submitted that an intermediary loses immunity where it plays an active role in promoting, optimizing, or facilitating access to infringing content, even if it does not directly sell or possess the goods. In L’Oréal v. eBay, the Court examined whether eBay could be held liable for trademark infringement committed by its users and recognized that the platform’s conduct went beyond mere passive hosting. Notably, eBay engaged in keyword advertising using trademarked terms, structured search results, and facilitated visibility of infringing goods through algorithmic and commercial tools. The Court highlighted that such conduct may amount to use of the trademark in relation to infringing goods, thereby attracting primary liability. Further, the scale of operations, the existence of filtering systems, and the platform’s knowledge of recurring infringement indicated that the intermediary was not merely a neutral conduit but an economically engaged actor benefiting from and enabling unlawful activity. This principle directly applies to the present case, where DeepSearch similarly structures and generates outputs through its AI architecture, and potentially facilitates the dissemination of unlawful content at scale. The active involvement in content generation and optimization, combined with foreseeable misuse, places DeepSearch outside the scope of passive intermediary protection and makes it liable for facilitating infringement.

Delfi AS v. Estonia

Explanatory Paragraph
It is submitted that an online platform may be held liable for third-party content where it exercises control over the platform environment, derives economic benefit from user engagement, and fails to prevent clearly unlawful content, even in the presence of a notice-and-takedown system. In Delfi AS v. Estonia, the European Court of Human Rights upheld the imposition of liability on a news portal for defamatory and threatening comments posted by users, despite the existence of disclaimers and a reactive takedown mechanism. The Court emphasized that the platform had integrated the comments section into its business model, actively encouraged user participation, and benefited economically from increased traffic generated by such comments. Importantly, the comments in question were manifestly unlawful, including threats and hate speech, which the platform ought to have prevented or removed proactively. The Court rejected the argument that the platform was merely a passive intermediary, holding instead that it exercised sufficient control over the publication environment and could determine which comments remained visible. This principle directly applies to the present case, where DeepSearch’s generative system not only facilitates but produces and disseminates harmful content at scale, and derives value from user engagement. Accordingly, the Respondent cannot rely solely on post-facto removal mechanisms to escape liability, particularly where the unlawful nature of the content is foreseeable and inherent in the system’s design.

Doe v. GitHub Inc.
Explanatory Paragraph (Principles)
It is submitted that liability for generative AI systems can arise not merely from output reproduction, but from systemic design choices in training and deployment that foreseeably result in copyright violations. In Doe v. GitHub Inc., plaintiffs alleged that AI models (Codex/Copilot) were trained on publicly available code governed by open-source licenses requiring attribution, yet were deliberately designed to generate outputs without preserving attribution, copyright notices, or license terms. The Court recognized that such allegations—particularly regarding the intentional removal or suppression of copyright management information (CMI)—were sufficient to sustain claims under Section 1202(b) of the DMCA. Crucially, the Court accepted that liability may arise where the system is designed in a way that facilitates infringement at scale, even if individual instances of harm are probabilistic. The Court also acknowledged that a “substantial risk” of reproduction of copyrighted material through AI outputs is sufficient to sustain injunctive claims. This principle directly applies to the present case: where DeepSearch’s generative system is trained on copyrighted material and produces outputs that are inherently violative upon distribution, liability can be attributed not only to specific outputs but to the architecture and training design itself, particularly where safeguards like attribution or filtering are knowingly omitted.

Spentex Industries Ltd. v. Commissioner of Central Excise
Explanatory Paragraph (Principle + Application)
It is submitted that statutory provisions must be interpreted purposively and harmoniously with the overall legislative scheme, rather than through a narrow literal interpretation that leads to absurd or unjust outcomes. In Spentex Industries Ltd. v. CCE, the Hon’ble Supreme Court rejected a purely literal reading of Rule 18 (which used the word “or”) and instead interpreted it in light of the broader legislative intent of granting full duty relief on exports, ultimately reading “or” as “and” to avoid anomalous results. The Court emphasized that interpretation must align with the object, policy, and scheme of the statute, and that even clear words may be reinterpreted where literal construction defeats legislative purpose. This principle is directly applicable to the present case, where provisions governing intermediary liability (such as Section 79 of the IT Act and IT Rules 2021) must be interpreted in light of their underlying objective—either to protect free speech and innovation (Respondent) or to prevent harm and regulate unlawful digital conduct (Petitioner). Therefore, the scope of safe harbour cannot be determined through a rigid literal reading but must be construed in a manner that furthers the legislative purpose in the context of evolving technologies like generative AI.

On reading “or” as “and”
Court can read “or” as “and” where necessary to give effect to legislative intent

On avoiding anomalous results

Indian Case Laws: Respondent
2. MySpace Inc. v. Super Cassettes Industries Ltd.
Explanatory Paragraph
It is submitted that intermediary liability arises only upon specific and actual knowledge of infringing content, and that the law does not impose a general obligation to monitor or pre-screen user-generated content. In MySpace v. Super Cassettes, the Hon’ble Delhi High Court held that “actual knowledge and not mere suspicion is essential to fasten liability,” thereby establishing that intermediaries are required to act only when specific infringing content is identified and brought to their notice. The Court further rejected the imposition of broad filtering or monitoring obligations, noting that such requirements would result in impermissible “private censorship.” This principle strongly supports the Respondent’s case that DeepSearch cannot be held liable merely because its system is capable of generating unlawful outputs. In the absence of specific notice identifying the impugned content, there is no obligation on the platform to act. However, the Petitioner may rely on this case in a limited manner to argue that once such specific knowledge is established and the intermediary fails to act, safe harbour protection is lost.
Key Extracts (Use in Memorial)
    • “Actual knowledge and not mere suspicion is essential to fasten liability.” 
    • “Providing long lists… would result in private censorship.” 
    • Obligation arises only when specific infringing content is identified 
    • Intermediary must act within prescribed time after notice 
3. Google LLC v. Visaka Industries
Explanatory Paragraph
It is submitted that intermediaries cannot be treated as publishers of third-party content and are liable only upon failure to act after receiving legally recognized actual knowledge. In the Google case, the Court clearly distinguished between the originator of content and the intermediary, observing that there was “no case… that the Appellant is the author or publisher” of the impugned material. The Court further clarified that “actual knowledge” must arise through a court order or appropriate government notification, thereby protecting intermediaries from liability based on informal or generalized complaints. Additionally, the Court cautioned against imposing proactive monitoring obligations, noting that such expectations would convert intermediaries into “private censors.” This principle is directly applicable to DeepSearch, which operates as a technological platform responding to user prompts and does not independently originate content. In the absence of formal notice and demonstrable authorship or control, DeepSearch retains its intermediary protection and cannot be held liable for user-generated outputs.
Key Extracts (Use in Memorial)
    • “There is no case… that the Appellant is the author or publisher…” 
    • “Actual knowledge must be… through a court order or Government agency.” 
    • Intermediaries cannot be turned into private censors 
    • Liability arises only upon failure to act after knowledge 
4. Kent RO Systems Ltd. v. Amit Kotak
Explanatory Paragraph
It is submitted that the law does not impose any obligation on intermediaries to proactively monitor, screen, or prevent the hosting of potentially unlawful content, and that liability arises only upon specific notice and demonstrable intent or participation. In Kent RO Systems Ltd. v. Amit Kotak, the Hon’ble Delhi High Court held that the IT Rules “do not oblige the intermediary to… screen all information being hosted,” and that imposing such a duty would effectively convert the intermediary into a body responsible for adjudicating complex legal questions. The Court further recognized that content hosting occurs “automatically, without human intervention,” and that requiring prior screening would “bring the business… to a halt.” Additionally, the Court clarified that liability under Section 79(3) arises only in cases of conspiracy, abetment, or aiding, which require proof of common intention and active participation. This principle strongly supports the Respondent’s position that DeepSearch, as an automated generative system, cannot be expected to pre-emptively evaluate or restrict all possible outputs, and in the absence of intentional facilitation or failure to act upon notice, safe harbour protection remains intact.

    • Hosting is “automatic, without human intervention.” 
    • Screening would “bring the business… to a halt.”

International Case Law: Respondent
Force v. Facebook
Explanatory Paragraph

It is submitted that intermediary immunity extends broadly to platforms even where they employ algorithmic tools to organize, recommend, and distribute third-party content, so long as they do not materially contribute to the illegality of the content itself. In Force v. Facebook, the Court held that Section 230(c)(1) bars claims that seek to treat platforms as publishers of third-party content, even where such content is amplified through personalized algorithms such as newsfeeds, friend suggestions, and targeted recommendations. The Court rejected the argument that algorithmic “matchmaking” or targeted dissemination removes intermediary protection, holding that such functions are inherent to publishing and do not transform the platform into a content creator. Crucially, the Court adopted the “material contribution” test, clarifying that a platform becomes liable only when it contributes to what makes the content itself unlawful, and not merely by making it more visible or accessible. This principle directly supports the Respondent’s case, as DeepSearch’s generative and recommendation-based architecture can be characterized as a technological process responding to user input rather than independently creating unlawful content. Therefore, absent proof that DeepSearch materially contributed to the illegality of the output itself, safe harbour protection remains intact.

Google LLC v. Oracle America Inc.
Explanatory Paragraph (Principle + Application)
It is submitted that the doctrine of fair use in the context of computer programs is flexible, context-driven, and must be applied in light of technological evolution and functional considerations. In Google v. Oracle, the U.S. Supreme Court held that copying portions of a copyrighted API (approximately 11,500 lines of code) constituted fair use, primarily because the use was transformative, limited to what was necessary, and enabled developers to apply their existing knowledge in a new technological environment. The Court emphasized that computer programs are inherently functional in nature, and therefore traditional copyright protections must be applied cautiously. It further recognized that fair use serves as a “context-based check” on copyright monopoly, ensuring that protection does not stifle innovation.
This principle is directly applicable to the present case: where generative AI systems are trained on vast datasets, the use of such material may be justified as transformative, particularly where the output serves a new purpose and does not merely replicate the original work. Accordingly, liability cannot be automatically imposed for training or limited use of copyrighted material, especially where such use is necessary for technological advancement and does not substitute the original work.

Sony Corp. of America v. Universal City Studios Inc.(Also called the Betamax case)
This is foundational for AI liability arguments, especially training + intermediary liability.

Explanatory Paragraph (Memorial-ready)
It is submitted that liability cannot be imposed upon a technology provider merely because its product is capable of infringing uses. In Sony Corp. of America v. Universal City Studios Inc., the U.S. Supreme Court held that the sale of a technology does not amount to contributory infringement where the technology is capable of substantial non-infringing uses. The Court recognised that home “time-shifting” of television programs constituted fair use, particularly because it was non-commercial and did not cause demonstrable market harm.

Further, the Court emphasized that copyright law must be interpreted in light of technological advancement, and that expanding liability to innovators would unjustifiably extend copyright monopoly beyond its statutory limits. Accordingly, where a technology serves legitimate and socially beneficial purposes, its mere potential for misuse cannot justify liability. This principle is directly applicable to generative AI systems, which, despite possible infringing outputs, are fundamentally capable of substantial lawful and transformative uses.

Key Doctrines from the Case
1. Substantial Non-Infringing Use Test (MOST IMPORTANT)
Technology is NOT illegal if it has lawful uses
“Sale of copying equipment… does not constitute contributory infringement if the product is… capable of substantial noninfringing uses.”

2. Time-Shifting = Fair Use
Recording TV to watch later = legal
Even though full work copied
Private, non-commercial use = fair use

3. No Automatic Liability for Intermediaries
Sony did NOT:
control users
directly infringe
So no contributory liability
4. Market Harm is Crucial
No proven harm → no infringement
No likelihood of “nonminimal harm” to market

5. Technology-Neutral Interpretation
Copyright must adapt to technological change
Balance:
creator rights
public access

Viacom Int'l, Inc. v. YouTube, Inc.
This case defines when online platforms are liable vs protected under the DMCA safe harbor.
It is submitted that online intermediaries are entitled to statutory protection where infringing content is uploaded by users, unless they possess actual or constructive knowledge of specific infringements. In Viacom Int’l v. YouTube, the Second Circuit clarified that the DMCA safe harbor under §512(c) applies where infringement occurs due to content stored at the direction of users, provided the platform lacks knowledge of specific infringing material and acts expeditiously upon notice.

The Court rejected the notion that a general awareness of infringement is sufficient to impose liability. Instead, it held that liability arises only where the platform has item-specific knowledge or engages in willful blindness toward particular infringing acts. The judgment further emphasized that service providers are not under a general obligation to monitor user activity, reinforcing the legislative intent to protect innovation in digital platforms.

Key Doctrines (Very Important for Moot)
1. DMCA Safe Harbor (§512(c))
Platforms are protected if:
No actual knowledge of infringement
No “red flag” awareness
Remove content quickly after notice
No direct financial benefit + control

2. Specific Knowledge Requirement (CRITICAL)
General awareness ≠ liability
Must know specific infringing content
Knowledge must be “specific and identifiable”
3. Actual vs Red Flag Knowledge
Type                    Meaning 
Actual knowledge	Subjective awareness
Red flag knowledge	Obvious infringement (objective test)
BOTH require specific infringement, not general suspicion
4. No Duty to Monitor
Platforms are NOT required to:
actively search for infringement
monitor all content
5. Willful Blindness Doctrine
If platform deliberately avoids knowledge → liability
“Willful blindness = knowledge”
6. “Right and Ability to Control”
Mere ability to remove content ≠ control
Must be something more than just hosting/removal power

Perfect 10, Inc. v. Amazon.com, Inc.
This case answers a crucial question:
When is a platform actually “displaying” or “distributing” copyrighted content online?

Memorial-Ready Explanation
It is submitted that an intermediary does not directly infringe copyright where it merely facilitates access to infringing content without hosting or transmitting the copyrighted work itself. In Perfect 10 v. Amazon, the Ninth Circuit held that a search engine does not “display” or “distribute” copyrighted images where it merely provides HTML links to third-party content. Instead, liability arises only where the defendant stores and transmits the infringing content itself, as clarified through the “server test.”

Key Doctrines (VERY IMPORTANT)
1. The “Server Test” (MOST IMPORTANT)
A platform is liable ONLY if:
It stores the copyrighted content, AND
It serves/transmits it to users

Court says:
If you only link → NOT infringement
If you host + send → infringement
Example:
Google thumbnails → stored → ✅ infringement
Full-size images → on third-party servers → ❌ no direct infringement

2. No Direct Liability for Linking
Providing HTML instructions ≠ displaying content
Google only gives address of image
User’s browser fetches it from another site
Therefore:
No “copy” is shown by Google

3. Distribution Right Clarified
Diribution requires:
Actual dissemination of a copy
Google:
Does NOT send images
Only enables access
Hence:
No distribution liability

4. Transformative Use (Fair Use Boost)
Search engines are highly transformative
Images used as search tools, not for original purpose
Adds new function (indexing/information retrieval)
Court:
Search engines provide “social benefit” and new purpose

5. Contributory Liability Still Possible
Platform can still be liable if:
Knows of infringement
Helps facilitate it
Fails to act
 But this is secondary liability, not direct
