Issue 2: Safe Harbour vs. Content Creator/Publisher Liability
Preliminary Framing
Before diving into sides, understand the structural tension at the heart of this issue. Section 79 of the IT Act was designed in an era of passive platforms — forums, hosting services, search engines. DeepSearch represents something categorically different: an AI system that doesn't merely carry content but synthesizes it. The entire debate pivots on whether the law can stretch to accommodate this new reality, or whether the court must declare the old framework inapplicable.
The issue also has a sub-question embedded within it that most participants will miss: even if DeepSearch qualifies as an intermediary for its general services, does the specific Personality Model feature attract a different classification? You should argue these as separable — the platform might be an intermediary, but the Personality Model feature is a content creation engine.

Detailed Argument Analysis

APPELLANT'S ARGUMENTS (Rachel / Petitioner)

A1: The Personality Model Is an Active Content Creation Engine — Not Passive Hosting
This is the foundational argument from which all others flow, and it must be established clearly before anything else.
The legal architecture of Section 79 rests on a tripartite functional classification: (i) the initiator, who originates electronic communication; (ii) the transmitter, who passively passes it along; and (iii) the intermediary, who provides a neutral conduit. Safe harbour belongs only to the third category. The moment an entity moves into category (i) or actively participates in the character of category (ii), the protection dissolves.
The critical question is: what does the Personality Model actually do? It takes 30 seconds of existing footage of a real, identified individual — footage it either accesses from the internet or is fed by a user — and it constructs a synthetic identity architecture. This architecture is not a passive database. It is a trained neural model that can generate novel audio-visual representations of the target person in any scenario, speaking any words, performing any acts. The Rachel Personality Model in DeepSearch's global library is not a record of Rachel — it is a simulation engine for Rachel.
The distinction matters enormously. A library hosting Rachel's actual videos is a passive intermediary. A platform offering a tool that generates new "Rachel" content from scratch is something entirely different — it is a manufacturer of synthetic identity outputs. The output is not user-generated in any meaningful sense. The user does not bring the content — the user merely provides a prompt, and the Personality Model provides Rachel's synthesized voice, face, mannerisms, and appearance. The creative and technical authorship of the output is overwhelmingly the platform's model, not the user's input.
Draw the analogy sharply: if a printing press is a neutral tool, and a ghostwriter is a service provider, the Personality Model is closer to a ghostwriter who impersonates a specific named person. You do not absolve the ghostwriter of responsibility by pointing out that a client commissioned the work. The ghostwriter who becomes the person and speaks in their identity is not a passive conduit.
The Delhi High Court's reasoning in Christian Louboutin v. Nakul Bajaj is directly applicable. The court held that an e-commerce platform that curates, certifies, and markets products — rather than passively hosting seller listings — becomes an active participant and loses safe harbour protection. Extend this reasoning: DeepSearch curates, trains, and actively provides a specialized identity-cloning service for specific named individuals. The Personality Model is not a general AI tool — it is a specifically configured replica of a named person, maintained in a persistent library. That specificity is the evidence of active participation.

A2: Section 79(2)'s Three Conditions Are All Violated
Section 79(2) makes safe harbour conditional on three requirements: (a) the intermediary must not have initiated the transmission; (b) it must not have selected the receiver of the message; and (c) it must not have modified the information contained in the transmission. The Personality Model violates all three on a careful reading.
On initiation: Initiation is typically understood as the act of originating the communication. DeepSearch did not merely receive a user request to host an existing video. It originated the capacity to generate synthetic Rachel content by building, training, and deploying the Rachel Personality Model. The initiation here is not the moment the user clicked "generate" — it is the moment DeepSearch constructed and listed the Rachel Personality Model in its global library, making it available to any user globally. That act of model-creation and library-listing is an independent act of initiation of a new form of identity-based synthetic content.
On selection: Traditional intermediaries do not select who receives information. DeepSearch's Personality Model, however, was built around a specific, selected target — Rachel Sharma. The selection is built into the architecture of the model itself. Unlike a general video-generation tool, the Personality Model is specifically indexed to Rachel's biometric identity. Every use of this model involves a selection — the selection of Rachel as the synthetic subject. That selectivity is exercised by DeepSearch in the model's design and maintenance, not by the end user.
On modification: This is perhaps the most powerful condition to argue. Modification under Section 79(2)(c) is traditionally understood as altering existing data in transmission. But the Personality Model goes far beyond modification — it constitutes wholesale synthetic fabrication. It does not take Rachel's real video and modify it. It takes biometric parameters derived from her footage and generates entirely new audio-visual content that never existed, in which she appears to do things she never did. This is not modification of existing information — it is creation of false information using her identity as raw material. If "modification" is interpreted purposively (as Indian courts consistently do in fundamental rights and beneficial legislation contexts), the creation of fabricated synthetic identity content using a real person's biometric data is the most extreme form of modification possible.

A3: Section 79(3) — Actual Knowledge + Failure to Act
Section 79(3)(b) provides that safe harbour does not apply where the intermediary, upon receiving actual knowledge that any information in its computer resource is being used to commit an unlawful act, fails to expeditiously remove or disable access to that information.
The facts are unambiguous. Indean authorities formally notified DeepSearch of: (i) the existence of the Rachel Personality Model; (ii) its specific misuse to create non-consensual sexual deepfake content targeting an Indean citizen; and (iii) the resulting criminal complaint. This constitutes actual knowledge in the clearest sense — not constructive knowledge, not algorithmic notice, but direct governmental notification of a specific harm.
DeepSearch's response? It refused to disable the Rachel Personality Model, citing its "terms of use" and its policy of "uninterrupted services." This is precisely the conduct Section 79(3) is designed to address.
The argument here must anticipate the respondent's likely counter — that "actual knowledge" under Shreya Singhal requires a court order, not a police or government notification. Address this head-on. Shreya Singhal struck down Section 66A partly because it did not require judicial oversight before content removal. But Section 79(3) is a liability provision, not a censorship provision. Reading Shreya Singhal to mean that private platforms can only be compelled to remove content by court order — and therefore can ignore direct governmental notice of ongoing criminal harm — would effectively immunize platforms from liability for all harms occurring during the litigation delay period. That cannot be the correct reading. The distinction is between the obligation to proactively monitor (which Shreya Singhal correctly rejected) and the consequence of inaction after receiving specific, credible notice of ongoing harm (which Section 79(3) addresses). DeepSearch received the latter and chose inaction.
Furthermore, the IT (Intermediary Guidelines) Rules 2021 impose an independent, specific due diligence obligation to ensure the platform is not used for impersonation (Rule 3(1)(b)(iii)), sexually explicit material without consent (Rule 3(1)(b)(i)), and content that causes harm to the user's dignity (Rule 3(1)(b)(iv)). These rules create a positive compliance obligation. DeepSearch's refusal to remove the Rachel Personality Model is a direct violation of these due diligence obligations, which independently strips safe harbour protection regardless of the Section 79(3) knowledge analysis.

A4: Failure of the Substantial Non-Infringing Use / Neutrality Test
Even on the respondent's best case — that DeepSearch is a general AI tool and the Personality Model is just a feature — the safe harbour defense fails because the Personality Model cannot satisfy the neutrality requirement that underlies all platform immunity frameworks.
The Sony v. Universal City Studios (Betamax) test established that a technology provider escapes liability if the product is capable of substantial non-infringing uses. This test has been absorbed into the intermediary liability frameworks of multiple jurisdictions and implicitly underlies the Indian safe harbour framework's premise of neutrality. The test requires both design neutrality and use neutrality.
The Personality Model fails both. On design: it is specifically engineered to clone a particular individual's identity from minimal data. Unlike a general video-editing tool or even a general AI image generator, the Personality Model's defining feature is its ability to generate synthetic representations of specific named, real people. This is not design-neutral — it is identity-targeted by architecture.
On use: what are the substantial non-infringing uses of a tool that clones a specific private individual's identity from 30 seconds of footage? Unlike deepfake technology generally (which has documented legitimate uses in film restoration, accessibility services, educational content), the Personality Model library — which indexes real, identified individuals — has a structural tendency toward misuse. The very construct of maintaining a library of real people's synthetic personas, accessible globally, creates a product whose primary commercial appeal lies in enabling users to generate content as if it were those real people. The substantial use is the infringing use.
Push this argument further with a market substitution analysis: the Rachel Personality Model's existence in DeepSearch's global library means that any user anywhere in the world can generate infinite Rachel-attributed content without her consent. This is not an incidental harm — it is a direct commercial offering (users pay for or access the Personality Model feature) built on the misappropriation of a real person's biometric identity. The "creative tool" framing collapses when the creativity being enabled is the creation of false realities about real people.

A5: The AI Co-Authorship Doctrine — Platform as Synthetic Co-Creator
This is the most jurisprudentially innovative argument for the appellant's side and the one most likely to generate judicial interest.
Traditional content liability law distinguishes between: (a) the author/creator who is fully liable; (b) the publisher who exercises editorial control and shares liability; (c) the distributor who passively disseminates and has limited liability; and (d) the conduit/carrier who has no liability. The question is which category AI-generated content falls into, and the answer requires building a new analytical framework.
When a user interacts with the Personality Model to generate a deepfake of Rachel, the authorship of the output is functionally shared between the user and the model in a legally meaningful way. The user's contribution is minimal — perhaps a prompt, a scenario description, or a trigger command. The model's contribution is the entirety of the synthetic representation: Rachel's face, voice, mannerisms, facial geometry, audio characteristics, and behavioral patterns — all derived from the model's training on her footage and synthesized through the model's generative architecture.
This is not like a user uploading a video to YouTube, where the platform's role is purely technical. The Personality Model is a co-creator in the functional sense — it contributes original synthetic content. In copyright law, we recognize that authorship attaches to creative contribution. Logically, if a party contributes the dominant creative element of harmful content, that party cannot claim the immunity of a passive conduit.
Frame this as the "functional authorship" test: liability under Section 79 should be assessed by asking who functionally created the harmful content, not just who formally "uploaded" it. Where AI-generated content derives the dominant elements of its harmful character — the identity of the victim, the synthetic representation of their face and voice, the believability of the fabrication — from a platform-provided model, the platform is a functional author and cannot claim safe harbour.
This argument also has implications beyond this case — it is an invitation to the Supreme Court to lay down a doctrine for AI-generated harm that Indian law currently lacks entirely. Courts in the US are grappling with this question in the Stability AI and Midjourney litigations. The Indian Supreme Court has the opportunity to be globally ahead of the curve.

A6: Design Defect Liability — The Product Was Built for Identity Exploitation
The final appellant argument draws from product liability law to fill the gap left by the intermediary liability framework. Even if Section 79 is read in DeepSearch's favor, a separate head of liability exists: the platform deployed a product with a design defect — specifically, the Personality Model's core design makes non-consensual identity exploitation not just possible but structurally inevitable.
A design defect exists when a product, used as intended, causes harm that a reasonable alternative design could have avoided. The Personality Model was designed to: (i) require only 30 seconds of footage — deliberately minimizing the barrier to identity cloning; (ii) persist in a globally accessible library — rather than a private, access-controlled environment; (iii) operate without identity verification or consent — no mechanism to confirm the cloned person authorized the model; (iv) provide no takedown mechanism — once created, the Personality Model cannot be removed at the subject's request.
Each of these design choices made identity exploitation more likely. Reasonable alternative designs exist and are straightforward: require verified consent from the cloned individual; limit library access to verified professional use cases; implement automated harm-detection for sexual content generation; provide the subject with takedown rights. The fact that DeepSearch chose none of these alternatives is evidence that the design was not merely negligently defective — it was optimized for frictionless identity replication regardless of consent.
This argument is particularly powerful because it addresses DeepSearch's contractual "terms of use" defense directly. Terms of use that prohibit harmful use of a product do not cure a design defect that structurally enables and incentivizes that harmful use. A car manufacturer cannot escape liability for a defective brake system by pointing to a clause in the owner's manual saying "do not crash your car." Similarly, DeepSearch cannot escape liability for a product architecturally designed to enable identity exploitation by pointing to a ToU clause that nominally prohibits misuse.

RESPONDENT'S ARGUMENTS (DeepSearch Inc.)

R1: Structural Intermediary Status — The Platform Is Not the Originator of Harm
DeepSearch's foundational defense is that it is structurally and functionally an intermediary within the meaning of Section 79 and the IT Act's definitional framework. The Act defines an "intermediary" in Section 2(1)(w) broadly as any entity that receives, stores, or transmits an electronic record on behalf of another. DeepSearch provides an AI platform — users access it, deploy its features, and generate outputs. The initiating act of harm is the user's decision to generate a sexual deepfake of Rachel. DeepSearch neither decided to target Rachel, nor commissioned the specific content, nor distributed it to the public. All three acts of harm were performed by Mike Rathore.
The logical structure of the appellant's case, if accepted, would collapse the distinction between tool and act. A kitchen knife is designed to cut; a person who uses it to stab someone commits the harm. The manufacturer's liability is governed by product safety law, not criminal or tortious imputation of the act itself. Similarly, DeepSearch provided a generative AI tool — the decision to clone Rachel's identity for a sexual deepfake was entirely Mike Rathore's. Imposing publisher liability on DeepSearch for this user decision would be the equivalent of treating every tool manufacturer as a joint tortfeasor for every foreseeable misuse of their tool.
The Viacom v. YouTube precedent from the US (applicable as persuasive authority) established that a platform is not liable for user-uploaded content unless it has specific knowledge of a specific infringing item and fails to remove it. The specificity requirement is important — general awareness that a platform can be misused is not sufficient. Prior to receiving notice of Mike's specific deepfake, DeepSearch had no specific knowledge of this particular harmful use. Its liability, if any, arises only at and after the point of notice — and even then, the question of what it was obliged to remove (the specific video, or the entire Personality Model?) is a question of proportionality, not absolute liability.

R2: The User Is the Content Creator — Tool Does Not Equal Act
This argument directly challenges the appellant's "AI co-authorship" doctrine. The Personality Model is a technology tool, not a content creator. It does not, unprompted, generate sexual content about Rachel. It requires a user to: access the platform, find or construct the Personality Model, choose to generate content using it, specify the nature of the content to be generated, and actively deploy the output. Every step in this chain is a human decision. DeepSearch's model is the instrument; Mike Rathore is the agent.
The co-authorship argument misconstrues what the Personality Model actually does. It synthesizes audio-visual characteristics — a technological function no different, in legal principle, from a photo-editing application that can manipulate images. The application does not decide to manipulate; the user does. The app does not decide what the manipulation will look like; the user instructs it. Holding the tool liable for the user's creative decisions would mean that Adobe is liable for every defamatory image created in Photoshop, and that Google is liable for every misleading document written in Google Docs.
The better analogy is not to a ghostwriter but to a professional voice actor's vocal replication software. Such technology has legitimate creative, accessibility, and entertainment applications. The fact that it can be misused does not make the software developer a co-author of every misuse. The liability framework must remain act-focused, not tool-focused.
Furthermore, under Indian copyright law, authorship attaches to the person who directs and controls the creative process. Even if one accepts that AI tools contribute technically to output, the direction, intent, and deployment of the Personality Model for the specific purpose of creating sexual content targeting Rachel was entirely Mike Rathore's. He is the author of the harm; DeepSearch is the instrument.

R3: Terms of Use as Good Faith Compliance and the Proportionality of Relief Sought
DeepSearch's Terms of Use explicitly prohibit: (i) the creation of non-consensual intimate content; (ii) the use of Personality Models to impersonate individuals without their consent; (iii) the distribution of harmful or defamatory synthetic content. These prohibitions demonstrate that DeepSearch took good faith steps to prevent misuse — steps that the IT (Intermediary Guidelines) Rules 2021 recognize as satisfying due diligence obligations.
The appellants seek not merely the removal of the specific deepfake video (which was accomplished) but the global deletion of the Rachel Personality Model from DeepSearch's library. This relief is disproportionate to the harm and inconsistent with the intermediary liability framework. The IT Act's framework, and the 2021 Rules, contemplate a notice-and-takedown mechanism — not a product recall or feature shutdown mechanism. Removing the specific harmful content is the appropriate and proportionate response. Demanding the deletion of the entire Personality Model — which may have legitimate uses for other users, such as Rachel using her own model for content creation — is akin to asking a video platform to delete all videos of a person because one user uploaded a defamatory video of them.
The contractual terms of use, while not a complete defense, demonstrate that DeepSearch has not facilitated harm willingly or without compliance mechanisms. Where a platform has acted in good faith and in compliance with applicable due diligence obligations, Section 79's safe harbour protection should be robustly maintained to avoid chilling the development of legitimate AI services.

R4: The Chilling Effect on AI Innovation and the Legislative Gap Argument
This argument operates at the level of legal policy and institutional competence. Imposing publisher liability on a generative AI platform for user-directed misuse of a tool would have severe and immediate consequences for the development of legitimate AI services accessible to Indean users. The safe harbour framework under Section 79 was enacted precisely to prevent this outcome — to allow technology platforms to operate without becoming insurers of all possible misuse.
Generative AI tools — image generation, voice synthesis, video synthesis — have documented legitimate applications in film restoration, dubbing and accessibility for persons with hearing impairments, digital art, historical reconstruction, educational content, and medical simulation. The Personality Model technology, in legitimate deployment, enables content creators, educators, and businesses to create synthetic presentations without requiring physical presence. Holding DeepSearch liable as a publisher would signal to all AI developers that offering user-facing generative tools in or accessible to Indea creates unlimited publisher liability — effectively foreclosing the AI services market.
The court should be particularly cautious about legislating through liability at a moment when the legislature is actively considering AI governance frameworks. The appropriate institutional response to the regulation of generative AI deepfakes is parliamentary legislation with carefully calibrated liability standards — not judicial expansion of a 2000-era statute that was not designed for AI-generated content. The IT Act was enacted before generative AI existed. Its framework should not be judicially retrofitted to impose publisher liability in a vacuum that the legislature has not yet addressed.

R5: The "Actual Knowledge" Standard Under Shreya Singhal — Notice Must Come Through Court
Shreya Singhal v. Union of India established a critical safeguard: platforms are not required to act as arbiters of legality when they receive government requests to take down content. The court held that intermediaries should be protected from government overreach and should not be required to remove content merely on the basis of a government or police request — the legitimacy of the takedown request must be judicially validated.
Applied here: DeepSearch received notification from Indean authorities — but this notification was a governmental request, not a court order. Under Shreya Singhal's framework, compliance with a non-judicial government takedown request is optional for intermediaries — the safe harbour is not forfeited merely because a platform declines to act on a government notice without judicial backing. The respondent would argue that by the time the HC granted an injunction, DeepSearch's obligation to act crystallized — and that its subsequent compliance with the injunction (had it been directed to) would satisfy Section 79(3).
This argument simultaneously defends DeepSearch's initial inaction and challenges the sufficiency of the appellant's Section 79(3) "actual knowledge" argument. The knowledge that grounds liability under Section 79(3) must be knowledge of specific unlawful content coupled with a failure to remove it after judicial direction. Prior to the injunction, DeepSearch's position — that it awaited judicial determination of whether the Rachel Personality Model violated Indean law — was not unreasonable.

R6: Section 79 Was Not Designed for Foreign Entities — A Structural Limitation
The final respondent argument is a structural one that challenges the entire application of Section 79 to DeepSearch. Section 79 operates within the framework of the IT Act, which — as the High Court correctly observed — has limited extraterritorial reach. If Section 75 (Issue 1) fails to ground jurisdiction, then Section 79 cannot be invoked against DeepSearch at all, because the Act's safe harbour provisions are addressed to entities subject to the Act.
Even if jurisdiction is established under Section 75, the question of whether Section 79's framework — which presupposes a platform operating within Indea's regulatory environment, complying with notice requirements, maintaining grievance redressal mechanisms under the 2021 Rules — can be both invoked against a foreign entity (to impose liability) while simultaneously being used as the standard of compliance (to assess safe harbour) is conceptually problematic. DeepSearch does not have a grievance officer in Indea, does not maintain the 2021 Rules' compliance architecture, and does not operate under the Indean regulatory framework. It cannot simultaneously be held to the liability standards of an Indean intermediary while being exempt from the compliance obligations that define intermediary status.
The correct outcome under this argument: either DeepSearch is outside the Act's jurisdiction entirely (in which case the court must find another legal basis for liability), or DeepSearch is within the Act's jurisdiction and subject to all its obligations — including the 2021 Rules' mandatory compliance requirements, violation of which independently grounds liability.

Strategic Notes for the Moot
The most important thing to internalize is that Issue 2 is not self-contained — it interacts deeply with Issue 1 (jurisdiction must be established before Section 79 applies) and Issue 3 (if personality rights exist under Article 21, they provide an independent basis for relief that bypasses the Section 79 safe harbour debate entirely). In oral arguments, be ready to pivot between issues when a judge pushes you on a weakness in one area.
For the appellant's side, your strongest arguments are A2 (conditions violated) and A5 (co-authorship doctrine) — lead with A2 because it's statute-based and hard to escape, and deploy A5 as your creative capstone.
For the respondent's side, your strongest argument is R2 (tool vs. act) combined with R4 (chilling effect on innovation) — these resonate with policy-minded judges and force the appellant to justify a rule that would reshape the entire AI industry.
